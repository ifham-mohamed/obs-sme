# CHAPTER 01 - INTRODUCTION

## 1.1 Introduction

Small and Medium Enterprises (SMEs) form the backbone of the Sri Lankan economy, contributing significantly to GDP, employment generation, and entrepreneurial innovation [1], [2]. Despite their economic importance, a persistent and largely unexamined challenge undermines their sustainability: regulatory non-compliance driven not by intent, but by information failure. Sri Lankan SMEs operate within a complex regulatory environment governed by multiple authorities, including the Inland Revenue Department (IRD), the Employees' Provident Fund (EPF), the Employees' Trust Fund (ETF), and the eRegistrar of Companies (eROC). Each of these institutions issues regulations, amendments, and procedural updates that SMEs are legally obligated to follow [3].

However, observation and preliminary inquiry suggest that the average SME owner in Sri Lanka does not fail at compliance because they wish to evade regulation. They fail because the right information does not reach them at the right time, in the right language, with the right level of accuracy. Regulatory changes published in the Government Gazette take weeks or months to reach SMEs. Guidance received from accountants, social media groups, and online forums is often inaccurate [4]. Official documents are predominantly in English, while most SME owners conduct business in Sinhala or Tamil [5]. By the time a penalty notice arrives, the owner often did not know a violation was occurring.

This research proposes a structured, empirical investigation into the information barriers that drive regulatory non-compliance among Sri Lankan SMEs. Rather than assuming the problem and building a tool, this study measures each barrier first - quantifying information lag, knowledge gaps, risk invisibility, and misinformation spread - and then constructs a unified, AI-powered platform validated against those measurements. The work contributes four novel datasets and four measurable findings to the body of knowledge on SME compliance in developing economies.  

1.2 Background and Motivation

### 1.2.1 The SME Sector in Sri Lanka

SMEs constitute over 75 percent of all enterprises in Sri Lanka and are widely recognized as a critical engine of economic development [1], [2]. They generate a substantial share of national employment, approximately 45 percent of the workforce, contribute around 52 percent to GDP, and play a vital role in regional economic balance by sustaining livelihoods outside major urban centers [1], [6]. The sector spans a diverse range of industries, including manufacturing, retail, agriculture, tourism, services, and construction [2]. SMEs are typically owner-managed, operate with limited administrative staff, and rarely possess in-house legal or compliance expertise [7].

This structural reality places Sri Lankan SMEs in a position of asymmetric vulnerability. Unlike large corporations, which maintain dedicated tax, legal, and compliance departments, SMEs depend on external advisors, informal networks, and self-research for regulatory understanding [4], [7]. The economic crisis of 2022 and subsequent tax reforms - including the introduction of the Social Security Contribution Levy (SSCL) under Act No. 25 of 2022 and revisions to the VAT regime - have intensified the regulatory burden on this sector [8], [9]. Yet the support infrastructure for navigating this burden has not evolved correspondingly.

### 1.2.2 The Regulatory Compliance Landscape

Regulatory compliance for a Sri Lankan SME involves obligations across multiple, fragmented authorities. The Inland Revenue Department administers Income Tax, Value Added Tax (VAT), Withholding Tax, and the Social Security Contribution Levy [3], [9]. The Employees' Provident Fund and Employees' Trust Fund regulate mandatory contributions for employee welfare. The e-Registrar of Companies governs incorporation, annual returns, and corporate filings. Sector-specific regulators add further layers - the Board of Investment, the Central Environmental Authority, the Consumer Affairs Authority, and others. Such fragmentation, common across developing economies, has been identified as a significant driver of compliance cost and complexity for SMEs [10].

Critically, regulatory changes in Sri Lanka are formally communicated through the Government Gazette, a publication that is publicly available but not actively pushed to affected businesses. There is no centralized SME-facing notification system. Updates to tax rates, filing deadlines, contribution thresholds, and procedural requirements may be published with little advance warning [8], leaving SMEs to discover changes through accountants, news media, social media, or - most commonly - penalty notices issued after the fact.

### 1.2.3 The Information Barrier Problem

The central motivation for this research is a recurring observation: Sri Lankan SMEs want to comply, but cannot, because of four interlocking information barriers.

The first barrier is timing. Regulatory changes do not reach SMEs quickly enough to act. The lag between gazette publication and actual SME awareness has never been measured for Sri Lanka, but anecdotal evidence and regional studies suggest it is significant [11].

The second barrier is accuracy. The compliance guidance SMEs receive - from accountants, peers, Facebook groups, and forums - is frequently incorrect or outdated [4]. The accuracy rate of these informal channels has never been benchmarked.

The third barrier is visibility. SMEs cannot see their own compliance risk until a violation occurs. There is no early warning system, and no study has identified which SME characteristics most strongly predict compliance failure in the Sri Lankan context [12].

The fourth barrier is language and integrity of information. Misinformation about tax regulations spreads rapidly through Sinhala and Tamil social media networks [13], [14]. The prevalence and virality of this misinformation, relative to accurate guidance, has not been quantified.

Together, these four barriers form an invisible compliance trap. This research seeks to make the trap visible - and then to dismantle it.

1.3 Problem Statement

### 1.3.1 Core Problem

Sri Lankan SMEs experience high rates of regulatory non-compliance not because of unwillingness, but because critical regulatory information fails to reach them in the right time, in the right form, in the right language, and with the right accuracy [4], [10]. Despite the economic significance of the sector [1], [2], no systematic, data-driven study has measured the information barriers that produce this outcome, and no integrated solution exists that addresses all four barriers in a unified manner.

### 1.3.2 Sub-Problems

The core problem decomposes into four investigable sub-problems, each corresponding to a distinct information barrier:

**Sub-Problem 1** - The Awareness Gap. Regulatory changes are published in the Government Gazette but reach SMEs only after a significant and unmeasured delay. The duration of this lag, the channels that bridge it, and the change-types most affected have never been empirically established [11].

**Sub-Problem 2** - The Knowledge Gap. The compliance guidance circulating among SMEs - through accountants, peers, and online sources - varies widely in accuracy [4]. The gap between official regulatory requirements and actual SME understanding has never been quantified, nor has the accuracy of informal guidance channels been benchmarked against verified sources.

**Sub-Problem 3** - The Risk Invisibility Gap. SMEs lack any mechanism for predicting their own compliance failure before it materializes. The characteristics, behaviors, and contextual factors that predict non-compliance in the Sri Lankan SME context have not been studied, leaving owners blind to risk until penalties are issued [12], [15].

**Sub-Problem 4** - The Misinformation Gap. Regulation misinformation spreads through Sri Lankan SME networks, particularly via WhatsApp, Facebook, and online forums [13], [14]. The prevalence of this misinformation, its rate of spread relative to accurate content, and the linguistic features that make it viral have never been measured.

## 1.4 Research Questions

### 1.4.1 Main Research Question

**_What are the information barriers that drive regulatory non-compliance among Sri Lankan SMEs, and how can they be systematically identified and addressed through a unified, AI-powered platform?_**

This question is genuinely unanswered in the existing literature, focuses on a real-world problem rather than on the performance of AI systems in the abstract, and requires investigation across all four research modules to be answered comprehensively. Its findings are generalizable to other developing economies that share Sri Lanka's regulatory fragmentation [10], [11].

### 1.4.2 Module-Specific Research Questions

To address the main question, four module-specific research questions guide the investigation:

RQ1 - Awareness Gap (Module 1): Are regulatory changes reaching Sri Lankan SMEs in time to act, and what is the information lag between Gazette publication and SME awareness?

RQ2 - Knowledge Gap (Module 2): How accurate is the compliance guidance that Sri Lankan SMEs receive, and what is the gap between official regulatory requirements and what SMEs actually understand?

RQ3 - Risk Gap (Module 3): Which characteristics of Sri Lankan SMEs predict compliance failure, and can these signals be detected before a violation occurs?

RQ4 - Misinformation Gap (Module 4): How significantly does regulation misinformation spread through Sri Lankan SME networks, and what makes certain misinformation more viral than accurate official guidance?

1.5 Aim and Objectives

### 1.5.1 Aim

The aim of this research is to empirically investigate the information barriers that drive regulatory non-compliance among Sri Lankan SMEs and to design, develop, and validate a unified, AI-powered SME Regulatory Intelligence Platform that delivers the right information, to the right SME, at the right time, in the right language - measurably reducing each identified barrier.

### 1.5.2 Objectives

The aim is achieved through the following objectives:

**Objective 1** - Measure the Awareness Gap. To quantify the information lag at every stage from Gazette publication to SME awareness, identify the most effective communication channels, and develop an automated gazette-monitoring and alert system validated against measured awareness times [16].

**Objective 2** - Measure the Knowledge Gap. To construct a verified ground-truth knowledge base of Sri Lankan compliance requirements, benchmark SME compliance knowledge against this ground truth, measure the accuracy of informal guidance channels, and develop a Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG) multilingual compliance assistant [17], [18] validated against the measured baseline using standard frameworks such as RAGAS.

**Objective 3** - Measure the Risk Gap. To identify the SME characteristics and behavioral signals that most strongly predict compliance failure, evaluate machine learning approaches under data-scarce conditions, and develop an explainable early-warning risk-scoring system using SHAP-based interpretability [12], [15] validated retrospectively against historical violation records.

**Objective 4** - Measure the Misinformation Gap. To quantify the prevalence, spread, and linguistic characteristics of regulation misinformation in Sri Lankan SME networks, construct an annotated misinformation corpus using cross-lingual transformer models such as XLM-R [5], [19], and develop a real-time claim verification interface validated against the corpus.

**Objective 5** - Integration and Contribution. To integrate the four module outputs into a single platform, demonstrate operational interoperability between modules, and deliver four novel datasets and four measurable research findings as scholarly contributions.

## 1.6 Scope and Limitations

Scope. The research is scoped to registered Small and Medium Enterprises operating within Sri Lanka, focusing on regulatory obligations administered by the IRD, EPF, ETF, and eROC [3]. Regulatory changes considered span the period 2018 to 2026, providing both pre- and post-economic-crisis coverage [8]. The research covers content in English, Sinhala, and Tamil [5]. The platform is developed as a research prototype, not a production-grade commercial deployment.

Limitations. Several limitations are explicitly acknowledged. First, no private enterprise data is collected; the study relies entirely on publicly available data, ethically administered surveys, and synthetically generated data calibrated to public statistics - meaning some findings are constrained by the granularity of public records. Second, survey-based measurements depend on respondent self-reporting and are subject to recall and selection bias, mitigated through sampling protocols but not eliminated. Third, social media data collection is constrained by platform API limits and terms of service, particularly on Facebook and X [13]. Fourth, translation of Sinhala and Tamil content using machine translation introduces possible semantic drift, which is mitigated by manual verification on a sample basis [5]. Fifth, the validation period is limited to the duration of the academic project, which restricts longitudinal observation of platform impact.

## 1.7 Stakeholders

The research engages and serves multiple stakeholder groups. SME owners and finance staff are the primary beneficiaries; they will use the platform to receive timely, accurate, and language-appropriate compliance guidance. Regulatory authorities - including the IRD, EPF, ETF, and eROC - benefit from improved voluntary compliance and are potential institutional partners for deployment [3]. Industry bodies such as the Ceylon Chamber of Commerce, the National Enterprise Development Authority (NEDA), and sector-specific chambers serve both as channels for survey distribution and as future dissemination partners [1]. Accountants and tax practitioners are simultaneously a subject of study (as a guidance channel whose accuracy is measured) and potential users of the verified knowledge base [4]. The academic community benefits from the four novel datasets and the generalizable methodologies released as research outputs. The Faculty of Information Technology at the University of Moratuwa and the project supervisor provide academic oversight, evaluation, and intellectual guidance.

## 1.8 Proposed Solution Overview

The proposed solution is the SME Regulatory Intelligence Platform, a unified, AI-powered system in which each component is empirically grounded in a measured information barrier rather than in assumption. The platform draws on the Regulatory Technology (RegTech) paradigm, which applies AI, NLP, and data analytics to streamline compliance [16], [20], adapted here for the data-scarce, multilingual context of a developing economy.

The platform integrates four interoperating modules. The Regulatory Change Monitor continuously scrapes the Government Gazette and official portals, classifies changes using a fine-tuned multilingual NLP model based on XLM-R [5], [19], and pushes timely alerts to registered SMEs in their preferred language. The Compliance Guidance Assistant is a RAG-based question-answering system [17], [18] grounded strictly in a verified knowledge base of official regulatory documents, providing accurate multilingual responses with citations to source authorities. The Compliance Risk Predictor assigns each SME an explainable risk score using a machine learning model trained on public violation records, survey data, and synthetic data, with SHAP-based explanations [12], [15] indicating the contributing risk factors. The Claim Verification Interface allows users to submit any compliance claim received from any source - WhatsApp message, Facebook post, accountant's advice - and receive an instant verdict cross-checked against the verified knowledge base [13], [14].

The four modules share a common verified knowledge base and trigger one another in response to real-world events. When the monitor detects a new regulation, the knowledge base updates, risk scores are recomputed, and the claim verifier begins evaluating new claims against the updated rules. The platform is delivered through a React.js frontend and a FastAPI backend, with PostgreSQL and ChromaDB providing structured and vector storage respectively.

Each module is validated not by user satisfaction surveys alone, but by direct comparison against the empirical baselines established in its own research investigation - closing the loop between the problem measured and the solution delivered.

## 1.9 Summary

This chapter introduced the central concern of the research: that Sri Lankan SMEs fail at regulatory compliance not from intent but from information failure, and that this failure operates through four distinct, measurable barriers - awareness lag, knowledge inaccuracy, risk invisibility, and misinformation spread. The chapter articulated the core problem and its four sub-problems, formulated the main research question and its four module-specific counterparts, defined the aim and five supporting objectives, and bounded the scope and limitations of the study. It identified the stakeholders who motivate, participate in, and benefit from the work, and outlined the SME Regulatory Intelligence Platform as the unified solution whose components are each grounded in an empirically measured barrier. Finally, it presented the structure of the dissertation that follows. The next chapter establishes the scholarly context for these contributions through a critical review of the relevant literature.

# CHAPTER 02 - LITERATURE REVIEW

## 2.1 Introduction

This chapter establishes the scholarly foundation for the present research by critically reviewing the literature across the seven domains that intersect at the research problem: SME regulatory compliance in developing economies, information asymmetry as a theoretical lens, regulatory communication and awareness lag, compliance knowledge gaps, machine-learning-based compliance risk prediction, misinformation detection in financial and regulatory contexts, multilingual NLP for low-resource languages, and retrieval-augmented generation (RAG) for domain-specific question answering. Each section examines what has been studied, identifies the limits of existing work, and isolates the gap that this study fills. The chapter concludes with a comparative analysis of existing approaches.

## 2.2 SME Regulatory Compliance in Developing Economies

A substantial body of research has examined the determinants of tax compliance behaviour among Small and Medium Enterprises (SMEs) in developing economies. Musiimenta et al. [22] established, in the Ugandan context, that knowledge requirements, tax complexity, and compliance costs significantly shape SME compliance behaviour, finding that SMEs with greater procedural understanding exhibit higher compliance rates. Similar results have been replicated across Indonesia, Botswana, Ethiopia, and Jordan: tax literacy, perceptions of fairness, and the quality of service delivery from tax authorities consistently emerge as the strongest predictors of voluntary compliance [23], [24], [25]. The slippery-slope framework proposed by Kirchler et al. [25] further argues that voluntary compliance depends on trust in authorities, while enforced compliance depends on authority power, and that the two reinforce one another.

While these studies firmly establish that information and understanding are the central levers of SME compliance, none explicitly measure the upstream information environment in which SMEs operate. They treat tax literacy as an attribute of the SME owner, not as a consequence of the regulatory communication channels available to them. The research consistently identifies that SMEs in developing economies struggle with disproportionate compliance costs and limited in-house capacity [7], [4], yet the question of how regulatory information actually flows from authorities to SMEs - and where it breaks down - remains unaddressed. The Sri Lankan literature in particular contains no systematic study that measures the channels, lag, or accuracy of regulatory information delivery for SMEs.

## 2.3 Information Asymmetry and Compliance Behavior

The theoretical framing for this research draws on the foundational work of Akerlof on information asymmetry [26]. In The Market for Lemons, Akerlof demonstrated how an imbalance of information between transacting parties can degrade market outcomes to the point of collapse. The framework has since been extended to a wide range of contexts, including pharmaceuticals [27], insurance, education, and financial markets, and has informed the design of regulatory signalling mechanisms intended to mitigate asymmetry [28].

Within the SME compliance literature, information asymmetry has been invoked primarily in the financial context - to explain why SMEs face restricted access to formal credit when lenders cannot verify their performance [21], [29]. ESG disclosure literature similarly argues that improved information disclosure reduces information asymmetry between SMEs and external stakeholders, lowering capital costs and improving compliance posture [29]. However, the asymmetry that this research investigates is of a different and largely underexplored type: the asymmetry between regulator and regulated. When a state publishes new regulations and the affected SMEs do not learn of them in time, in their language, or with accuracy, the asymmetry is not between a buyer and a seller but between a rule-maker and a rule-follower. This form of regulatory information asymmetry has been theoretically gestured at [10], [16] but never empirically measured for a developing-economy SME population. The present study positions itself directly within this gap.

## 2.4 Regulatory Change Communication and Awareness Lag

The Regulatory Technology (RegTech) literature has emerged in the past decade as the primary scholarly response to growing compliance complexity. Arner et al. [11] traced the post-2008 emergence of RegTech as a distinct subfield, motivated by the observation that compliance teams in large institutions spend significant resources continuously screening and interpreting regulatory updates. McKinsey's survey of the field [16] characterises RegTech as software that automates and improves how organisations manage compliance, monitor risk, and report to regulators, with each update reflecting current regulatory requirements. Butler and O'Brien [20] further demonstrated how semantic technologies and natural language processing can produce machine-readable regulatory taxonomies, enabling the automated propagation of regulatory change.

Recent advances apply transformer-based NLP directly to regulatory monitoring. Banerjee [30] describes a near-real-time regulatory awareness pipeline in which PDFs are ingested, legal entities extracted using fine-tuned transformers, and updates semantically linked to prior rules. Similar pipelines have been deployed in financial reporting [31] and in U.S. federal compliance contexts [32], with reported outcomes including a 75% faster turnaround for assessing regulatory impacts. Specialized work on regulatory question answering (RegNLP) has further explored the use of transformer architectures to extract obligations from legal contracts and EU/UK legislation [33].

The clear limitation of this literature is its near-exclusive focus on large, well-resourced institutions in advanced economies. RegTech is built and benchmarked for global banks managing GDPR or MiFID II compliance, not for an SME owner in Sri Lanka attempting to track gazette amendments. Crucially, none of these systems has been evaluated against a measured baseline of how long it actually takes for a regulatory change to reach the affected community. The lag from gazette publication to SME awareness is implicitly assumed to be a problem worth solving, but it has never been quantified for any developing-economy population. This research directly measures that lag.

## 2.5 Compliance Knowledge Gaps and Guidance Accuracy

The hypothesis that SME compliance failures stem largely from gaps in compliance knowledge has been empirically supported across many jurisdictions. Studies in Indonesia, Botswana, and Uganda consistently show that SMEs with higher tax literacy demonstrate measurably higher compliance [22], [23], [24]. Yet these studies measure knowledge as a self-reported attribute of the owner - typically through Likert-scale questions - rather than benchmarking actual SME knowledge against verified ground truth. They establish that knowledge matters, but they do not quantify the gap between what SMEs know and what regulations actually require.

A second strand of literature examines the sources SMEs consult for compliance guidance. SMEs in developing economies depend disproportionately on accountants, peers, and informal networks because they lack in-house compliance expertise [7], [4]. The accuracy of these informal channels has been subject to almost no empirical scrutiny. While the public-health literature has produced a rich body of work on informal health information accuracy on social media [34], [35], no equivalent body of work exists for regulatory information aimed at SMEs. The closest analogue is the broader misinformation literature reviewed in Section 2.7, but that work focuses on viral political and health misinformation, not on routine regulatory guidance.

Recent advances in retrieval-augmented generation (Section 2.9) provide a technical pathway for grounding compliance guidance in verified official sources, but the empirical baseline against which such systems should be evaluated - namely, the actual accuracy of currently available informal guidance - has not been established. This study constructs both the verified ground truth and the empirical baseline.

## 2.6 Compliance Risk Prediction Using Public Data

Machine learning for credit and compliance risk prediction has matured into a well-developed research area. Bussmann et al. [12] proposed an explainable AI model for credit risk that applies correlation networks to Shapley values, demonstrating on a dataset of 15,000 SMEs that risk profiles can be grouped according to similar financial characteristics. Subsequent work has applied SHAP- and LIME-based interpretability to credit-default prediction in Italian SMEs [36], to credit card fraud detection [37], and to broader financial decision support [38]. Lundberg and Lee's foundational SHAP framework [15] has become the de facto standard for explaining ML predictions in regulated financial domains, where regulators increasingly require auditability of automated decisions [39].

Two methodological challenges dominate this literature. The first is class imbalance: compliance violations and frauds are statistically rare, and naïve classifiers default to predicting the majority "compliant" class. The Synthetic Minority Over-sampling Technique (SMOTE) introduced by Chawla et al. [40] remains the most widely used remedy, with extensive empirical validation across credit-card fraud [41], [42], banking transactions, and healthcare. The second challenge is data availability: most published risk-prediction models rely on proprietary financial data from banks or fintech firms, making them difficult to replicate in jurisdictions without such data infrastructure.

For SMEs in developing economies - and Sri Lanka in particular - neither rich proprietary datasets nor large historical violation corpora are publicly available. The literature offers no model for compliance risk prediction that operates entirely on public data sources, calibrated through synthetic generation against population statistics. The closest parallel work uses synthetic data libraries such as CTGAN to simulate financial datasets, but always in addition to a substantial real dataset. This research designs a methodology specifically for the data-scarce, public-only setting characteristic of developing-economy SME populations.

## 2.7 Misinformation Detection in Regulatory and Financial Domains

The detection of misinformation on social media has produced an extensive literature centred on health misinformation (especially during COVID-19) and political misinformation. Balakrishnan et al. [14], in a systematic review of misinformation sharing on social media, identified WhatsApp and Facebook as the platforms most heavily implicated in the spread of rumours, with anxiety, political affiliation, and conspiracy ideation among the strongest predictors of sharing behaviour. Nobre, Ferreira, and Almeida [13] provided detailed network-oriented analysis of misinformation propagation on WhatsApp, demonstrating that despite WhatsApp's ostensibly private structure, an underlying network connects users across groups in ways that systematically favour information dissemination - and that misinformation campaigns leverage this structure for significant social, political, and economic effect.

Technically, misinformation detection has converged on transformer-based approaches. BERT and its variants have set the state of the art for fake news classification on benchmark English datasets [43], [44]. For multilingual settings, XLM-R and RemBERT have proven robust for cross-lingual transfer [45], with PolyTruth [45] and similar studies confirming that high-resource languages benefit from data abundance while low-resource languages still pose challenges. In financial and corporate contexts, FinSage and similar systems combine misinformation detection with retrieval-augmented verification [46].

What this literature lacks is any work specifically addressing tax or regulatory misinformation in a developing-economy SME context. Tax misinformation is mentioned only tangentially, typically as a subcategory in broader political-misinformation studies. No annotated corpus of Sri Lankan tax misinformation exists; no study has measured the prevalence, virality, or linguistic features of tax misinformation in Sinhala or Tamil social media. The cross-lingual modelling techniques are mature, but the data and the empirical study are absent.

## 2.8 Multilingual NLP for Low-Resource Languages

The capacity to process Sinhala and Tamil text with reasonable accuracy is a prerequisite for every module of the proposed platform. The dominant approach in this space is multilingual transformer models. Conneau et al. [19] introduced XLM-R, a transformer pre-trained on over 100 languages, and demonstrated significant performance improvements on low-resource languages compared to earlier multilingual models. In the Sri Lankan context specifically, Dhananjaya et al. [5] published the first comprehensive analysis of pre-trained language models for Sinhala text classification, showing that XLM-R substantially outperformed alternative multilingual models such as LaBSE and LASER, and remained robust under limited fine-tuning data. They also released SinBERT, a RoBERTa-based monolingual Sinhala model that further outperforms multilingual baselines on Sinhala-specific tasks.

Subsequent work has applied these models to financial-domain Sinhala text. The hybrid multilingual sentiment analysis framework of Sandaruwan et al. [47] achieved 75.9% accuracy on Sinhala sentiment classification using XLM-R-large, while the keyword extraction and aspect classification pipeline of Wijesiri et al. [48] reported 87.4% accuracy on Sinhala financial text using a fine-tuned XLM-R augmented with domain-specific vocabulary. Similar results have been replicated in low-resource African contexts [49].

While the technical capability for Sinhala and Tamil NLP is now demonstrably mature, almost no work has applied it to regulatory or compliance text in Sri Lanka. The available models are evaluated on news classification, sentiment analysis, and machine translation; the regulatory and tax domain remains essentially unexplored. The adaptation of multilingual transformers to Sri Lankan tax regulation classification and misinformation detection is a contribution this research makes.

## 2.9 Retrieval-Augmented Generation for Domain-Specific QA

Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG), introduced by Lewis et al. [17], combines a parametric language model with a non-parametric retrieval mechanism to reduce hallucination and ground responses in external knowledge. The approach has been rapidly adopted in legally and regulatorily sensitive domains where factual grounding is mandatory. Wiratunga et al. [18] proposed CBR-RAG, integrating case-based reasoning with RAG for legal question answering, and demonstrated that structured retrieval substantially improves answer quality on legal benchmarks. FinSage [46] extends RAG to financial filings, addressing the inherent heterogeneity of regulatory documents (text, tables, diagrams) and the evolving nature of compliance standards. A telecom-specific RAG pipeline for radio regulations [50] and a hybrid legal-QA agent integrating RAG with multi-model ensembling [51] have further extended the approach to high-stakes regulatory settings.

RAGAS, introduced by Es et al. [52], has emerged as the de facto evaluation framework for RAG systems, providing reference-free metrics for faithfulness, answer relevancy, context precision, and context recall. ARES [53] and VERA [54] offer complementary evaluation methodologies. Trustworthiness surveys [55] have begun to formalise dimensions such as factuality, robustness, and transparency for RAG in production.

Two limitations are important for this research. First, the available legal and regulatory RAG systems are almost entirely English-language and trained on advanced-economy regulatory corpora - U.S. HIPAA, EU GDPR, U.S. SEC filings. None is adapted to Sri Lankan tax law, Sinhala/Tamil text, or the specific structure of the Sri Lankan Government Gazette. Second, the literature consistently evaluates RAG systems against curated ground-truth benchmarks but rarely against the real-world alternative information sources users would otherwise consult. The empirical question of whether a RAG system meaningfully outperforms the informal channels SMEs currently rely on has not been answered. This study answers it.

## 2.10 Comparative Analysis of Existing Approaches

The literature reviewed above can be organised along two dimensions: the _barrier_ it addresses, and the _context_ in which the work has been conducted. Table 2.1 summarises this analysis.

**Table 2.1 — Comparative Analysis of Existing Approaches Against the Four Information Barriers**

|   |   |   |   |
|---|---|---|---|
|**Barrier**|**Most Mature Existing Approach**|**Context Where Validated**|**Limitation in Sri Lankan SME Context**|
|Awareness lag|RegTech regulatory monitoring pipelines [11], [16], [30]|Large global banks; advanced economies|Built for institutions with compliance staff; never measured against a population's actual awareness lag; English-only|
|Knowledge gap|Tax literacy surveys [22], [23], [24]; RAG-based legal QA [17], [18], [46]|SME surveys in Africa/Asia; legal QA on English benchmarks|Knowledge measured as self-report, not against ground truth; RAG systems not evaluated against informal-channel accuracy|
|Risk invisibility|Explainable ML credit risk models with SHAP [12], [15], [36]|Italian/European SMEs with proprietary financial data|Requires proprietary data unavailable for Sri Lankan SMEs; not adapted to compliance (vs credit) prediction|

What this comparative view reveals is that the technical building blocks for each module already exist - but they exist in isolation, evaluated on different problems, in different languages, in different economies. Critically, none of the existing approaches has been instantiated for Sri Lankan SMEs, and none has been evaluated against a measured baseline of the barrier it claims to address. The literature offers methods; it does not offer findings.

## 2.11 Identified Research Gap

Synthesising the limitations identified across the preceding sections, this research addresses a distinct and currently unfilled gap in the literature. That gap can be expressed as four interlinked deficiencies.

First, no empirical measurement exists of the four information barriers - awareness lag, knowledge gap, risk invisibility, and misinformation spread - for Sri Lankan SMEs or for any comparable developing-economy SME population. The compliance literature studies knowledge as a self-reported attribute; the RegTech literature assumes lag is a problem; the risk-prediction literature requires proprietary data; the misinformation literature ignores tax content and Sinhala/Tamil-speaking communities. The result is that interventions are proposed and built without any measured baseline of the problem they are intended to solve.

Second, no annotated datasets exist for the Sri Lankan regulatory context. There is no published information-lag dataset, no compliance-knowledge benchmark, no SME violation-prediction dataset built from public data, and no annotated tax-misinformation corpus. The absence of these datasets blocks both reproducible measurement and reproducible model development.

Third, no integrated solution addresses all four barriers in a unified manner, with each component validated against the empirical measurement of the barrier it targets. The literature contains separate, disconnected systems - a regulatory monitor here, a legal RAG there, a credit-risk model elsewhere - but none that demonstrates closed-loop interoperability or that closes the loop between problem measurement and solution validation.

Fourth, no methodology has been published for compliance risk prediction in a public-data-only, multilingual, data-scarce regulatory environment. The methodological innovations required to operate under these constraints - synthetic calibration against public statistics, cross-lingual transformer adaptation to regulatory text, RAG grounded in public-document ground truth - have not been combined or generalised into a transferable framework for other developing economies.

This research addresses all four deficiencies simultaneously: it produces the empirical measurements, releases the annotated datasets, builds and validates the integrated solution, and articulates the generalizable methodology.

## 2.12 Summary

This chapter has reviewed the literature across the seven domains that intersect the research problem and has demonstrated that, while the technical and theoretical foundations for each module of the proposed platform are mature in isolation, no prior work has measured the four information barriers in the Sri Lankan SME context, no annotated datasets exist for these barriers, and no integrated solution has been validated against measured baselines. The compliance literature establishes that information matters but does not measure the information environment. The RegTech literature builds monitors but for large advanced-economy institutions. The risk-prediction literature requires proprietary data unavailable in Sri Lanka. The misinformation literature ignores tax content and South Asian languages. The RAG literature provides the architecture for grounded question answering but lacks Sri Lankan tax adaptation and lacks evaluation against the informal channels SMEs actually use.

The research gap this study fills is therefore both empirical (the four measurements), data-related (the four datasets), system-level (the integrated platform), and methodological (the transferable framework for data-scarce regulatory environments). The next chapter sets out the mixed-methods research design through which these contributions will be produced.

## 3.1 Introduction

This chapter documents the technology stack adopted for the SME Regulatory Intelligence Platform. The chapter is organised in three layers. Section 3.2 presents the **common technologies** — the cross-cutting infrastructure shared across two or more modules and forming the foundation of the platform. Sections 3.3 to 3.6 then descend into each individual module, articulating the module's specific technical requirements, indicating which common technologies it inherits and how they are configured for that module's purpose, and recommending module-specific technologies with credible alternatives. Section 3.7 makes the inter-module dependencies explicit and Section 3.8 closes the chapter.

For every technology selected, the rationale rests on four criteria: (i) demonstrated suitability for the task in published literature or industry practice; (ii) availability as open-source or free-tier software, given the academic nature of the project; (iii) compatibility with multilingual (English, Sinhala, Tamil) processing where relevant; and (iv) maturity sufficient to support reproducible research. Where a primary choice is recommended, alternative options are listed not as fallbacks but as legitimate substitutes that another team — or a future extension of this work — could justifiably adopt.

## 3.2 Common Technologies

This section documents the cross-cutting technologies shared across two or more modules. Each module section that follows references back to this section by component name when it inherits a common technology, indicating any module-specific configuration applied on top.

### 3.2.1 Data Acquisition

**Web Scraping.** The primary scraping framework is **Scrapy**, chosen for its asynchronous architecture, built-in throttling and retry semantics, and production-grade middleware support. For dynamic, JavaScript-rendered pages — a common requirement on Sri Lankan government portals that progressively load gazette listings — **Playwright** is layered on top of Scrapy through the _scrapy-playwright_ integration. **BeautifulSoup4** is used for lightweight, one-off parsing tasks where Scrapy's full project structure would be overhead. Alternatives considered include **Selenium** (rejected as primary due to higher resource cost), **Puppeteer** via _pyppeteer_ (less actively maintained for Python), and **httpx + selectolax** (recommended for static-HTML-only tasks where higher throughput is needed).

**PDF Parsing.** The primary parser is **PyMuPDF (fitz)** for native-text extraction, chosen for its speed, accurate text-coordinate recovery, and robust handling of multi-column gazette layouts. **pdfplumber** complements it for tables, where its layout-analysis algorithms outperform PyMuPDF. For scanned or image-only PDFs, **Tesseract OCR** (via _pytesseract_) is used with Sinhala and Tamil language packs installed. Alternatives include **PyPDF2 / pypdf** (simpler API but weaker layout handling), **pdfminer.six** (slower than PyMuPDF), **Camelot** (table-specialised), and **EasyOCR** or **PaddleOCR** as deep-learning-based alternatives to Tesseract.

**Survey Instrumentation.** The primary platform is **Google Forms**, selected for free unlimited responses, native Sinhala and Tamil rendering, easy distribution via WhatsApp and email, and direct export to Google Sheets. Alternatives include **KoBoToolbox** and **ODK Collect** for offline collection during in-person SME visits, **LimeSurvey** (open-source, self-hosted), and **Qualtrics** if the University of Moratuwa academic licence is accessible.

### 3.2.2 Natural Language Processing

**Transformer Models.** The primary multilingual model is **XLM-RoBERTa (XLM-R)**, accessed via the **HuggingFace Transformers** library. XLM-R is selected because the published literature establishes it as the strongest multilingual model for Sinhala text classification [5], with robust performance on low-resource cross-lingual transfer [19]. For tasks requiring monolingual Sinhala depth, **SinBERT** is used as a complementary model. Alternatives include **mBERT** (older, weaker on Sinhala), **LaBSE** and **LASER** (better for sentence-level similarity), **IndicBERT** and **MuRIL** (strong on Tamil), and **mT5** (encoder-decoder; suitable for generation).

**Information Extraction.** **spaCy 3.x** is the primary tool for named-entity recognition, dependency parsing, and rule-based extraction patterns, with custom pipelines trained on annotated regulatory entities (regulation IDs, dates, amounts, authorities). Alternatives include **Stanza**, **Flair**, **GLiNER** (zero-shot extraction without fine-tuning), and **regex + dateparser** for purely rule-based extraction over well-structured documents.

**Translation Pipeline.** The primary translation service is the **Google Cloud Translation API**, chosen for its mature Sinhala and Tamil support and reliable batch throughput. All machine-translated output is sample-verified by native-speaker team members, with disagreement rates tracked as a quality metric. Alternatives include **DeepL API**, **Azure Translator**, **NLLB-200** (Facebook's open-source 200-language model, deployable locally and strongly recommended for sensitive content), and **IndicTrans2** (superior Tamil quality but no Sinhala support).

### 3.2.3 Application Stack

**Backend.** The primary backend framework is **FastAPI**, selected for its asynchronous request handling, automatic OpenAPI documentation, and native Pydantic data validation that pairs naturally with the rest of the Python ML stack. **Uvicorn** is the ASGI server. Background and scheduled tasks run on **Celery** with a **Redis** broker. Alternatives include **Django REST Framework** (heavier but provides built-in admin UI and ORM), **Flask** (simpler but lacks FastAPI's modern features), and **Litestar**. For scheduling, **APScheduler** is a lightweight alternative to Celery suitable where Redis is not justified.

**Frontend.** The primary frontend stack is **React.js** (with TypeScript) styled using **TailwindCSS**, with **shadcn/ui** for accessible, composable components. **React Query** manages server state and **Recharts** is used for data visualisations within the platform UI. Alternatives include **Next.js** (recommended where SSR is needed), **Vue 3** with **Nuxt**, **Svelte / SvelteKit**, and **Streamlit** or **Gradio** for rapid internal dashboards.

**Databases.** The primary relational store is **PostgreSQL**, chosen for its maturity, JSON support, full-text search capabilities, and the **pgvector** extension that allows the same database to serve as a fallback vector store. **Redis** is used for caching, rate limiting, and the Celery broker. Alternatives include **MySQL / MariaDB**, **MongoDB** (recommended where document-oriented storage of scraped raw data dominates), **SQLite** (development), and **TimescaleDB** (PostgreSQL extension recommended for time-series workloads).

### 3.2.4 Annotation and Statistical Tooling

**Label Studio** is the primary annotation platform across modules requiring human labelling, chosen for multi-user support, multilingual interface, and flexible label schemas. **Cohen's Kappa** (two annotators) and **Krippendorff's Alpha** (more than two) are computed via the _nltk_ and _krippendorff_ Python packages for inter-annotator agreement. **pandas**, **NumPy**, and **scipy.stats** form the standard analytical toolkit; **Plotly** is used for interactive visualisations within the platform and **matplotlib / seaborn** for publication-quality figures in the dissertation. Alternatives for annotation include **Doccano** (lighter-weight), **Prodigy** (commercial), **brat**, and **INCEpTION**.

# CHAPTER 04 - OUR APPROACH

## 4.1 Introduction

This chapter presents the research approach adopted for the SME Regulatory Intelligence Platform. This chapter defines what we actually do: the conceptual framework that ties the four investigations together, the per-module approach that operationalizes each research question, the integration logic that makes the four modules a single platform, and the evaluation strategy that closes the loop between measured problem and validated solution.

The defining characteristic of the approach is its measure-first, build-second discipline. For each information barrier, the empirical measurement of the barrier comes before the construction of the solution, and the solution is then validated against that same measurement. This avoids the common failure mode of building tools that assume a problem rather than addressing one.

## 4.2 Conceptual Framework

The conceptual framework is illustrated by a simple chain that every module follows independently and that the integrated platform realizes collectively.

**Public Data Sources → Empirical Measurement of an Information Barrier → Novel Dataset and Quantified Finding → Targeted AI-Powered Intervention → Validation Against the Measured Baseline**

Four parallel chains — one per information barrier — converge on a single platform whose four components share a verified knowledge base and trigger one another in response to real-world events. The framework treats AI as an _investigative instrument_: NLP is used to measure information lag, RAG is used to measure knowledge accuracy, ML is used to surface invisible risk, and cross-lingual classifiers are used to measure misinformation spread. The same techniques are then re-deployed as the user-facing intervention, ensuring methodological coherence between investigation and solution.

Two principles govern the framework. First, **no private enterprise data**: every measurement and every model is built from publicly available data, ethically administered surveys, or synthetic data calibrated to public statistics. Second, **language equity**: every user-facing component supports English, Sinhala, and Tamil, because the language barrier is itself one of the information barriers being addressed.

## 4.3 Module 1 - Regulatory Change Awareness Gap

### 4.3.1 Users, Inputs, Outputs, Process

**Users:** SME owners and finance staff who need to know about regulatory changes the moment they occur; researchers who need the lag dataset.

**Inputs:** the Sri Lankan Government Gazette archive (2018–2026); secondary publication dates from IRD, EPF, ETF, and eROC portals; first-mention dates from the Daily FT, LBO, and Daily Mirror archives; SME survey responses indicating _when_ and _through which channel_ respondents learned of specific recent regulatory changes.

**Outputs:** the regulatory-change lag-timeline dataset; the per-channel effectiveness ranking; the trained change-classification model; the deployed automated gazette monitor that issues alerts in the user's preferred language.

**Process:** scrape and parse the gazette PDFs continuously; record portal and news mentions as they appear; reconcile each event back to its originating gazette publication; merge with survey-reported awareness times; compute the lag at every stage; and dispatch real-time alerts ahead of that measured awareness baseline.

### 4.3.2 Methodology

The investigation is structured as a four-stage temporal study. **Stage A — Source Monitoring** establishes a continuous scraping pipeline running on an hourly schedule for the gazette portal and daily for news archives. **Stage B — Event Reconciliation** links each gazette publication to its downstream appearances on official portals and in news media, producing a per-event timeline. **Stage C — Awareness Measurement** uses the SME survey to attach a third timestamp to each event — the date the average SME owner reports having become aware. **Stage D — Lag Analysis** computes the distribution of lags at each transition (gazette → portal, portal → news, news → SME), tests for differences across change type, sector, SME size, and region using the Kruskal–Wallis test and ranks communication channels by their median time-to-inform.

The change-classification model is trained on the corpus of scrapped gazettes labelled by change type. A TF-IDF + Logistic Regression baseline is compared against a fine-tuned XLM-R model; the comparison itself is reported as a finding because no prior baseline exists for Sri Lankan regulatory text classification. Validation of the deployed alert system is a direct comparison: the system's end-to-end delivery time per change must be measurably shorter than the measured average awareness lag from Stage C, with statistical significance.

4.4 Module 2 - Compliance Knowledge Accuracy Gap

### 4.4.1 Users, Inputs, Outputs, Process

**Users:** SME owners and finance staff seeking trustworthy answers to compliance questions; researchers needing the compliance Q&A benchmark.

**Inputs:** official IRD, EPF, ETF, and eROC documents that constitute the verified ground truth; survey responses from SMEs to a 40–50 question compliance-knowledge test; compliance claims extracted from public Facebook groups, Reddit Sri Lanka, and forums.

**Outputs:** the verified compliance knowledge base; the compliance Q&A benchmark dataset; the measured compliance-knowledge gap score for Sri Lankan SMEs; the measured accuracy rate of informal guidance channels; the deployed multilingual RAG-based compliance assistant.

**Process:** ingest official documents, structure them into citable chunks, and have a chartered accountant verify each entry; administer the survey and score responses against ground truth; scrape informal channels and verify their claims against the same ground truth; build the RAG assistant on the verified knowledge base and benchmark it against the measured informal-channel accuracy.

### 4.4.2 Methodology

The methodology proceeds in three phases.

**Phase 1** — Ground-Truth Construction ingests the corpus of official compliance documents, segments them into semantic chunks with source citations, structures the result as Markdown with YAML metadata in a Git-controlled repository, and routes each entry through a Label Studio verification template handled by an external chartered accountant. Inter-expert agreement is measured on a double-verified subset.

**Phase 2** — Gap Measurement administers the compliance-knowledge survey with branching by SME sector. Each response is scored against the ground truth, producing per-domain accuracy rates (e.g., VAT registration, EPF contribution, withholding tax). In parallel, claims scraped from informal channels are verified against the same ground truth and the resulting accuracy rate becomes the empirical baseline against which the RAG assistant must improve.

**Phase 3** — System Construction and Validation builds the RAG assistant using a hybrid retrieval strategy (dense embeddings via multilingual-e5-large combined with BM25, fused via reciprocal rank fusion), with citations rendered through LangChain's RetrievalQAWithSourcesChain. Evaluation uses RAGAS for faithfulness, answer relevancy, context precision, and context recall, and the system's accuracy is compared against the measured informal-channel baseline using a held-out test set drawn from the survey questions.

## 4.5 Module 3 - Compliance Risk Invisibility

### 4.5.1 Users, Inputs, Outputs, Process

**Users:** SME owners who need a forward-looking signal of their own compliance risk; advisors and chambers who need to identify vulnerable members.

**Inputs:** IRD published defaulter lists; SME-related court judgments from LawNet; sector-level financial-health indicators from CBSL annual reports; the SME vulnerability survey; synthetic SME profiles calibrated to Department of Census and Statistics aggregates.

**Outputs:** the SME vulnerability and violation dataset; the trained explainable risk-prediction model; SHAP-based feature-importance findings; the measured detection lead time at 1, 3, and 6 month horizons; the deployed risk-scoring service.

**Process:** assemble a unified labelled dataset from all five sources, calibrate synthetic profiles against public statistics, address class imbalance, train and compare baseline and advanced models, interpret the best model with SHAP, perform temporal lead-time analysis, and validate retrospectively against held-out historical violations.

### 4.5.2 Methodology

The methodology rests on a deliberately public-only data stance — no proprietary financial records are used at any point. Real violation records and court judgments form the backbone of the labelled minority class; the SME vulnerability survey contributes self-reported failures with rich contextual features; CBSL indicators provide sector-level macro features; SDV's CTGAN generator produces synthetic profiles whose marginal distributions are validated against Census aggregates.

Class imbalance is addressed in two stages: first by experimenting with class-weighted loss functions, then by applying SMOTE variants where weighting alone is insufficient. Four model classes are trained and compared — Logistic Regression as the interpretable baseline, Random Forest, XGBoost, and an LSTM that ingests temporal filing-history sequences. The best-performing model is interpreted using TreeSHAP (for tree models) or DeepSHAP (for the LSTM), producing both global feature-importance findings and local per-SME explanations.

Temporal analysis evaluates predictive performance at 1-, 3-, and 6-month lead times before each historical violation, answering the research question of how early a signal becomes detectable. Retrospective validation holds out the most recent year of IRD defaulter records and measures the model's ability to identify those defaulters from their pre-violation profiles.

## 4.6 Module 4 - Regulatory Misinformation Spread

### 4.6.1 Users, Inputs, Outputs, Process

**Users:** SMEs receiving compliance claims through informal channels who need to verify them instantly; researchers requiring the annotated misinformation corpus.

**Inputs:** public posts from Facebook groups, X, and Reddit containing tax claims; FactCheck.lk's pre-labelled claims; anonymised forwarded WhatsApp messages submitted via the survey with consent.

**Outputs:** the annotated Sri Lankan tax-misinformation corpus; the measured prevalence of misinformation in SME networks; the comparative classifier benchmark (fine-tuned XLM-R vs RAG verification vs hosted LLM); the virality-feature findings; the deployed real-time claim-verification interface.

**Process:** collect tax-claim posts at scale through compliant API access; translate Sinhala and Tamil content; manually annotate accuracy categories with measured agreement; analyse spread patterns by comparing engagement of accurate versus inaccurate content; train and compare three classifier alternatives; identify linguistic features predictive of virality; and correlate misinformation spikes with the regulatory-change timeline produced by Module 1.

### 4.6.2 Methodology

Collection proceeds through compliant API channels only — Meta Graph API for Facebook, X API v2 for tweets, PRAW for Reddit. WhatsApp content enters exclusively through user-submitted forwards with consent, recognising that scraping E2E-encrypted content is neither legal nor possible. Translation uses the Google Cloud Translation API with sample verification by native speakers; disagreement rates are tracked and reported.

Annotation uses Label Studio with a four-way schema: accurate, partially accurate, misleading, false. A double-annotated 20% subset is used to compute Cohen's Kappa (and Krippendorff's Alpha where three annotators participate); annotation only proceeds at scale once agreement clears 0.7.

The classifier comparison treats the choice of architecture as a research question in its own right. A fine-tuned XLM-R is benchmarked against RAG-based verification (which reuses the Module 2 knowledge base) and against a hosted-LLM upper bound. Each is evaluated on the same held-out test split using F1 and per-class precision and recall. Spread analysis builds a network of post-share relationships using NetworkX and compares engagement metrics of accurate versus inaccurate content. Virality prediction uses XGBoost on engineered linguistic features, with SHAP identifying which features drive virality. The temporal correlation with Module 1 is tested using cross-correlation analysis between regulatory-change events and misinformation-volume spikes.

## 4.7 Integration - The Unified Platform

The four modules are integrated into a single platform through a shared verified knowledge base and a set of inter-module triggers. The verified knowledge base built in Module 2 is the canonical source of truth; Module 1 uses it to label new gazette content, Module 4 uses it to verify user-submitted claims, and Module 3 references it when explaining which regulation a predicted risk relates to. When Module 1 detects a new regulatory change, three downstream actions fire automatically: the knowledge base is updated, Module 3 recomputes risk scores for affected SMEs, and Module 4 begins verifying new claims against the updated rules. The misinformation corpus produced by Module 4 feeds back into Module 1 as a signal of which regulatory topics most urgently require communication.

Architecturally, each module exposes its outputs through FastAPI endpoints that the React frontend composes into a single user experience. The user does not interact with four tools but with one platform whose internal structure reflects the four investigations.

## 4.8 Evaluation Strategy

The evaluation strategy is explicitly tied to the measure-first principle. Each module is validated _against the same measurement that originally established the problem_, eliminating the gap between assumed and demonstrated impact.  
  

|   |   |   |
|---|---|---|
|**Module**|**Empirical Baseline**|**Validation Test**|
|1|Average SME awareness lag measured by survey|System delivery time per change vs measured baseline; Wilcoxon signed-rank test|
|2|Accuracy rate of informal guidance channels|RAG assistant accuracy on held-out questions vs informal-channel baseline; RAGAS faithfulness ≥ 0.85 target|
|3|Historical IRD defaulter records held out|Retrospective recall and precision at 1, 3, and 6 month lead times|
|4|Annotated misinformation corpus held-out test split|Per-class F1 across three classifier alternatives; statistical significance of best vs second-best|

In addition to module-level validation, an integration test verifies the cross-module triggers fire correctly: a synthetic regulatory-change event is injected and the propagation through knowledge-base update, risk recomputation, and verifier readiness is checked end-to-end. A small-scale user evaluation with SME owners (n ≈ 15–20) provides qualitative feedback on usability, with the System Usability Scale administered to produce a comparable score.

## 4.9 Summary

This chapter has set out the research approach: a conceptual framework that converts each information barrier into an empirical measurement, a novel dataset, an AI-powered intervention, and a validation against the same baseline. Each of the four modules was specified in terms of its users, inputs, outputs, process, and methodology, and the integration logic that makes the four a single platform was made explicit. The evaluation strategy ensures that every claim of impact is grounded in the same measurement framework that defined the problem. ## 5.8 User Interface Design

The user interface is designed around a single dashboard rather than four separate tools, reflecting the platform's claim of unified intelligence

The design follows three principles. Language-first: a prominent language selector in the top bar applies to every component, including alerts and verdicts. ## 5.9 Inter-Module Communication Design

Inter-module communication uses an event-driven publish/subscribe model on Redis Pub/Sub, with a small set of well-defined event types. Modules do not call one another's APIs directly; they publish events and subscribe to events of interest. This decoupling allows any module to be developed, tested, and deployed independently.

**Table 5.4 — Inter-Module Events**

|   |   |   |   |
|---|---|---|---|
|**Event**|**Publisher**|**Subscriber(s)**|**Payload**|
|_change.detected_|M1|M2, M3, M4|change_id, change_type, gazette_number, publication_date|
|_kb.updated_|M2|M4|kb_entry_ids[], updated_at|
|_risk.recomputed_|M3|UI (via WebSocket)|profile_id, new_score, delta|
|_verdict.generated_|M4|(logging only)|claim_id, verdict_label, confidence|

When _change.detected_ fires, Module 2's KB ingestion job picks up the change, integrates the new regulation into the verified knowledge base after expert verification, and publishes _kb.updated_. Module 3's batch rescoring job picks up the same event and recomputes risk scores for SMEs whose profile features intersect with the change. Module 4 listens for _kb.updated_ to refresh its retrieval cache, ensuring the verifier never operates on stale ground truth. WebSocket connections to the React UI propagate _risk.recomputed_ events so users see updated scores in real time.

The event bus is intentionally simple. For the research prototype, Redis Pub/Sub is sufficient. A future production deployment could substitute Apache Kafka or NATS for guaranteed delivery and replay, without changing the publisher/subscriber contracts.

CHAPTER 06 - IMPLEMENTATION

## 6.1 Introduction

This chapter describes the planned implementation approach for the SME Regulatory Intelligence Platform. This chapter sets out how the implementation will be carried out: the development environment that will be standardized across the team, the planned construction sequence for each module, the integration strategy for binding the four modules into a unified platform, and representative pseudocode showing the structure of the most important components.

The implementation strategy will follow a vertical-slice incremental approach. Rather than building each module to completion sequentially, the team will build a thin end-to-end slice through every module first — a working scraper, a minimum viable knowledge base, a baseline classifier, a baseline risk model, and a working verifier — and will then iterate on each slice toward the full specification. This approach guarantees that the integration plumbing between modules is exercised early, that the platform can demonstrate end-to-end behavior at every milestone, and that integration risk is surfaced at the start of the project rather than at the end.

## 6.2 Development Environment and Setup

The development environment will be standardized across all four team members. Python 3.11 will serve as the runtime for all backend and ML code, with project dependencies pinned in a single _requirements.txt_ and environment isolation through venv. Node.js 20 LTS will support the React frontend, with npm for faster installs and stricter dependency resolution. PostgreSQL 16 with the TimescaleDB and pgvector extensions will run locally via Docker Compose; the same Compose file will provision Redis 7, ChromaDB, and a Label Studio instance, allowing a new team member to bring up the entire stack with a single _docker compose up_ command.

Source control will be managed on a private GitHub organisation under _enigmatrix-uom/sme-regulatory-platform_, with one branch per active feature and pull requests reviewed by at least one other team member before merging. Continuous integration will run on GitHub Actions: every push will trigger _pytest_ for backend unit tests, _eslint_ and _vitest_ for frontend tests, and _ruff + black_ for Python linting and formatting. Pre-commit hooks will enforce the same checks locally to keep CI fast. Experiment tracking for ML training runs will use MLflow on a self-hosted server within the university network, with run artefacts stored on the local filesystem.

Ethics approval will be obtained from the University of Moratuwa Faculty of Information Technology research committee before any survey distribution commences. Survey participants will be recruited through the Ceylon Chamber of Commerce, NEDA, and LinkedIn outreach, with informed consent captured electronically as the first page of every survey instrument.

## 6.3 Module 1 Implementation Progress

### 6.3.1 Gazette Scraper

The gazette scraper will be implemented as a Scrapy project with _scrapy-playwright_ middleware to handle the JS-rendered listing pages on _documents.gov.lk._ The scraper will retrieve the gazette index, download each linked PDF, and store the file along with the publication date and gazette number in the _regulatory_changes_ table. A separate spider will handle the IRD, EPF, ETF, and eROC notice pages; each will be implemented as a _CrawlSpider_ subclass with site-specific link extractors.

A back-fill of the gazette archive from January 2018 onward will be performed once to seed the dataset, after which the scraper will run on an hourly schedule via a Celery beat task. Two engineering risks are anticipated. First, naive scraping is likely to trigger server-side throttling on government portals; this will be mitigated upfront by implementing a custom Scrapy _DOWNLOADER_MIDDLEWARE_ that enforces a one-second minimum delay between requests to the same host, with exponential back-off on HTTP 429 responses. Second, gazette filename schemes are known to be inconsistent across years; this will be handled by a year-aware filename normaliser written after a brief survey of naming conventions across the archive.

### 6.3.2 Regulatory Change Classifier

Two classifier variants will be implemented and compared. A **TF-IDF + Logistic Regression** baseline will be trained on a hand-labelled set of gazette excerpts across approximately twelve change-type categories such as _tax_rate_change, deadline_change, threshold_change_, and _procedural_amendment_. Class imbalance is expected to be significant, with procedural amendments and tax-rate changes likely to dominate; this will be addressed using class-weighted loss.

A **fine-tuned XLM-R** classifier will then be trained on the same labelled set to test whether transformer-based classification meaningfully improves on the baseline — a comparison that is itself a research finding given the absence of any prior baseline for Sri Lankan regulatory text classification. Early stopping on validation loss will be configured to mitigate overfitting on the small initial training set. The labelled set will be expanded incrementally by team members through ongoing labelling sessions, with inter-annotator agreement spot-checked.

### 6.3.3 Lag Computation

The lag-computation logic will be implemented as a SQL view over the _regulatory_changes, change_appearances_, and _awareness_responses_ tables, joined on _change_id_. The view will expose per-change lag durations at each transition (g_azette → portal, portal → news, news → SME_), plus an _overall_lag_ column representing the gazette-to-SME-awareness duration. Awareness data will be collected through the SME survey distributed to a target sample of approximately 200 respondents.

## 6.4 Module 2 Implementation Progress

### 6.4.1 Knowledge Base Construction

The verified knowledge base will be structured as a Git-controlled repository of Markdown files with YAML front-matter, with one file per regulatory section (for example, _kb/ird/income-tax/personal-relief.md_). Each file will declare its _source_doc, section, language, effective_date_, and _verified_by_ in the front-matter and contain the canonical text content. The ingestion pipeline will read this directory tree, chunk each file using a 512-token window with 64-token overlap, compute embeddings using multilingual-e5-large, and write both the chunks and the embeddings.

Expert verification will be performed by an external chartered accountant under a memorandum of understanding to be signed before knowledge-base construction begins. Entries will be routed to the accountant through Label Studio with a custom verification template that allows comments and corrections. The ingestion pipeline will be designed to be **idempotent** from the outset: re-running it after content changes will update only the affected entries by comparing content hashes, avoiding the cost of re-embedding the entire corpus on every run.

### 6.4.2 RAG Pipeline

The RAG pipeline will be implemented in **LangChain** following the design in Section 5.4.2. The hybrid retriever will combine dense embedding similarity (via the same _multilingual-e5-large_ model used for ingestion) with BM25 lexical search over the chunk text, fused using **reciprocal rank fusion** with k=60. Retrieved context will be passed to the LLM along with a system prompt that mandates citation of source authorities and refuses to answer when the retrieved context does not adequately cover the question.

The LLM backbone will be a hosted commercial LLM (such as Claude or GPT-4) for the prototype, with **Llama 3 8B Instruct** configured as the open-source alternative for the reproducibility benchmark planned for the final report. The chain will return answers with structured citations conforming to LangChain's RetrievalQAWithSourcesChain output, and these citations will be rendered inline in the React UI as clickable links to the source document section.

Evaluation will use the **RAGAS** framework, measuring faithfulness, answer relevancy, context precision, and context recall on a held-out test set drawn from the survey questions. Hyperparameter tuning experiments will be conducted on chunk size (256, 512, 1024 tokens) and number of retrieved chunks (k = 4, 6, 8) to find the configuration that maximises faithfulness while maintaining adequate context recall.

### 6.4.3 Compliance Knowledge Survey

The compliance knowledge survey will be deployed as a Google Form with branching by SME registration status (VAT-registered vs not, employer vs sole-trader) to ensure every respondent receives only relevant questions. The survey will contain 40–50 verifiable questions across five domains: VAT, income tax, EPF/ETF, SSCL, and corporate filings. Each question will have a single correct answer that can be objectively scored against the verified knowledge base. The target sample is approximately 100 valid responses.

A scoring engine will be implemented in Python to produce per-respondent and per-domain accuracy scores. The aggregate accuracy will become the empirical baseline of informal-channel and self-knowledge accuracy against which the RAG assistant must improve.

6.5 Module 3 Implementation Progress

### 6.5.1 Dataset Assembly

Five data sources will be combined into a unified labelled dataset. The IRD published defaulter list will be scraped and deduplicated with entity resolution (matching corporate name variants such as "(Pvt) Ltd" vs "(Private) Limited"). LawNet judgment records will be scraped and parsed to extract structured outcomes (violation type, fine amount, business sector). CBSL annual reports will be parsed to extract sector-level financial-health indicators over time. The SME vulnerability survey will collect self-reported compliance failures with rich contextual features. Finally, synthetic SME profiles will be generated using SDV's CTGAN model and calibrated against Department of Census and Statistics SME population marginals (sector × employee-count band × region), with calibration verified by chi-square tests against the public statistics.

The unified dataset is expected to exhibit a heavy class imbalance between violators and non-violators, which the modelling stage will address.

### 6.5.2 Model Training and Comparison

Four model classes will be trained and compared, all registered in MLflow for reproducibility. **Logistic Regression** will provide an interpretable baseline. **Random Forest** and **XGBoost** will provide strong tabular alternatives, with XGBoost hyperparameters tuned via optuna. An **LSTM** ingesting per-SME filing-history sequences will provide the temporal model needed to answer the lead-time research question.

Class imbalance will be addressed in two stages. First, class-weighted loss functions will be tried as the lightest-touch intervention. Where weighting alone proves insufficient, **SMOTE** oversampling will be applied at the feature-engineering stage, with **Borderline-SMOTE** and **SMOTEENN** held in reserve as variants. The temporal lead-time analysis will evaluate predictive performance at 1-, 3-, and 6-month horizons before each historical violation, answering the research question of how early a signal becomes detectable. Retrospective validation will hold out the most recent year of IRD defaulter records and measure the model's ability to identify those defaulters from their pre-violation profiles.

### 6.5.3 Explainability Layer

The explainability layer will use **SHAP**, with _TreeExplainer_ for the tree models and _DeepExplainer_ for the LSTM. The risk-scoring API endpoint will return both the numerical score and the top contributing features with their SHAP values, formatted as a structured JSON response that the React UI will render as a horizontal bar chart with risk-increasing and risk-decreasing factors visually distinguished. This explainability layer is not optional — it is the mechanism by which the platform converts an opaque score into actionable guidance for the SME.

6.6 Module 4 Implementation Progress

### 6.6.1 Social Media Collection

Collection will proceed exclusively through compliant API channels. Reddit will be collected via PRAW from r/srilanka and related subreddits, filtered to posts mentioning regulatory keywords. Facebook will be collected via the Meta Graph API from named public groups focused on Sri Lankan business and tax topics. X (Twitter) will be collected via the X API v2 under the Academic Research access tier, for which the team will apply early in the project. WhatsApp content, given its end-to-end-encrypted private nature, will be collected exclusively through user-submitted forwards via the survey instrument with explicit consent — there is no API option, and no scraping route would be either legal or possible. FactCheck.lk will be scraped to provide a high-quality seed of pre-labelled regulatory claims.

### 6.6.2 Annotation Workflow

Label Studio will be configured with a four-way label schema (accurate / partially accurate / misleading / false). At least three annotators will participate: two team members and one external native-Sinhala-speaking volunteer, with a fourth Tamil-speaking annotator to be recruited. Annotation guidelines will be drafted before annotation begins and refined based on early disagreement patterns, particularly around the harder middle categories.

A double-annotated 20% subset will be used to compute Cohen's Kappa (and Krippendorff's Alpha where three annotators participate). Annotation will only proceed at scale once agreement clears 0.7.

### 6.6.3 Misinformation Classifier

Three classifier variants will be trained and compared on the same held-out test split. The fine-tuned XLM-R will be the primary classifier, consistent with the platform's transformer choice and supported by published evidence on multilingual misinformation. A RAG-based verification variant will reuse the Module 2 knowledge base and retrieval pipeline, testing the hypothesis that grounding outperforms fine-tuning on regulatory claims — a comparison that is itself a research finding because it speaks directly to the data efficiency of the two approaches. A hosted-LLM variant (such as Claude or GPT-4 with a structured verification prompt) will provide the upper-bound baseline against which the open-source results are interpreted.

The three variants will be evaluated using per-class F1, precision, and recall, with statistical significance tested between the best and second-best variants.

## 6.7 Platform Integration Progress

The vertical-slice integration target is to have a working end-to-end demonstration by the end of the first development sprint. The FastAPI backend will expose endpoints for all four modules, the React frontend will render the unified dashboard described in Section 5.8, and JWT-based authentication will protect the routes. End-to-end behaviour to be demonstrated includes: a user registering, configuring their language and sector preference, viewing the latest gazette-detected alerts, asking a compliance question and receiving a cited answer, viewing their risk score with SHAP explanations, and submitting a claim for verification.

The Redis Pub/Sub event bus will be wired in early to exercise cross-module triggers from the start. An integration test will be written that injects a synthetic regulatory-change event into the bus and verifies downstream knowledge-base update, risk recomputation, and verifier readiness — providing automated assurance that the cross-module triggers behave as designed. WebSocket propagation of risk.recomputed events to the React UI will allow users to see updated scores in real time.

The alert dispatch channels will be implemented in priority order: email via SendGrid first (lowest friction), WhatsApp via the Meta WhatsApp Business API second (requires Meta business-account verification, planned to be initiated early to absorb the verification lead time), and SMS via Twilio third behind a feature flag pending budget approval for production-scale messaging during the user evaluation phase.
